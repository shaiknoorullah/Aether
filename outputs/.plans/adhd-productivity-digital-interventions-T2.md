# Researcher Brief T2: Employment Outcomes, Earnings & Occupational Functioning — Adult ADHD (2020–2026)

## Your job
Search for peer-reviewed evidence on how adult ADHD affects **employment status, job retention, career advancement, and earnings** compared to non-ADHD controls. Focus on 2020–2026; pre-2020 landmark studies acceptable as context.

## Key questions to answer
1. What is the employment rate / unemployment rate differential for adults with ADHD vs population controls?
2. What is the earnings gap between adults with ADHD and matched controls? (Studies reporting wage, salary, income differences)
3. Are there registry-based or large-cohort studies (n > 1,000) from 2020–2026 on occupational outcomes?
4. What occupational sectors/roles do adults with ADHD cluster in or avoid? Any evidence of "ADHD-compatible" job self-selection?
5. Does pharmacological treatment (stimulants) improve employment outcomes? Any natural experiment or quasi-experimental evidence?
6. What role does comorbidity (anxiety, depression, substance use) play in employment outcomes?

## Search strategy
Use paper search AND web search. Try queries:
- "adult ADHD employment outcomes longitudinal study"
- "ADHD earnings income wage gap"
- "ADHD occupational functioning registry study"
- "ADHD work outcomes stimulant medication employment"
- "ADHD labor market outcomes"
- "ADHD job tenure turnover career"

## Output format
Write a structured research note to: `outputs/.drafts/adhd-productivity-digital-interventions-research-T2.md`

Include:
- For each finding: claim, source title, authors, year, journal, URL or DOI
- Sample sizes and study design (registry, RCT, cohort, cross-sectional)
- Confidence level (HIGH/MEDIUM/LOW)
- Key statistics with direct quotes where possible
- Comorbidity adjustments noted
- All search queries used
- Mark URLs as UNVERIFIED if not confirmed to load

## Critical rules
- Do NOT fetch PDF URLs — use abstract/DOI landing pages
- Do NOT invent citations or data
- Record NOT FOUND if a specific paper cannot be located
- Prefer large-n, peer-reviewed sources
