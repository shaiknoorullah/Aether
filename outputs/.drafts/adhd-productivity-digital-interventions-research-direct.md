## Papers Collected

### T1 DOMAIN: Economic Burden, Productivity, Presenteeism

**1. Chhibber A et al. 2021 — Global Economic Burden of ADHD: Systematic Review**
- PMID: 33554324 | DOI: 10.1007/s40273-020-00998-0 | Pharmacoeconomics
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33554324/ — VERIFIED
- n=44 studies; all high-income, mainly N. America + Europe
- Per-person total costs: $831–$20,538; national estimates: $356M–$20.27B (USA)
- Marginal costs per person: $244–$18,751
- Confidence: HIGH (systematic review, pre-registered, quality-assessed)
- Caveat: Wide heterogeneity in methods; no LMIC data

**2. Merino M et al. 2024 — Socioeconomic Burden of Adult ADHD in Spain**
- PMID: 38601072 | DOI: 10.33393/grhta.2024.2697 | Glob Reg Health Technol Assess
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38601072/ — VERIFIED
- Annual cost per diagnosed adult: €15,652; one-time cost: €7,893
- National (Spain) annual: €3,035M; one-time: €1,531M
- 50% of annual cost from economic domain; 53% of that from work absenteeism
- 28% social domain; 74% of that substance-abuse-related
- Confidence: MEDIUM (economic model, bottom-up, expert elicitation; no prospective data)

**3. Daley D et al. 2019 — Danish Sibling Comparison Cost Analysis**
- PMID: 31288209 | DOI: 10.1016/j.eurpsy.2019.06.011 | Eur Psychiatry
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31288209/ — VERIFIED
- n=460 sibling dyads from Danish national registers
- Adults with ADHD cost an average €20,134/year MORE than their sibling (lower disposable income, higher state benefits, health, social care, crime costs)
- Year of data: 2010. Sibling design controls for genetics/shared environment
- Confidence: HIGH (matched-sibling design, register-based)

**4. Schein J et al. 2023 — ADHD Symptoms, QoL, and Work Productivity (US survey)**
- PMID: 36082503 | DOI: 10.1080/03007995.2022.2122228 | Curr Med Res Opin
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36082503/ — VERIFIED
- n=585 US adults on ADHD medications; online survey, July 2021
- 95.2% experienced ≥1 symptom past month (mean 5.8 symptoms)
- Number of symptoms significantly correlated with reduced QoL, reduced employment probability, and increased work/activity impairment
- 50.4% with insomnia reported "a lot" or "extremely" negative impact on well-being
- Confidence: MEDIUM (cross-sectional survey, self-report, industry-affiliated recruitment)

**5. Naya N et al. 2021 — Burden of Undiagnosed ADHD in Japan (WPAI)**
- PMID: 34956750 | DOI: 10.7759/cureus.19615 | Cureus
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34956750/ — VERIFIED
- n=9,816 respondents from Japan National Health and Wellness Survey 2016
- ASRS-positive undiagnosed adults showed significantly greater WPAI and HRU, lower HRQoL than ASRS-negative
- Multiple mental and physical comorbidities elevated
- Confidence: MEDIUM (cross-sectional, ASRS screening not full diagnosis, industry-funded Takeda/Shionogi)

**6. Ishimoto Y et al. 2023 — Working From Home and ADHD Symptoms (COVID-19)**
- PMID: 37484117 | DOI: 10.2147/NDT.S414862 | Neuropsychiatr Dis Treat
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37484117/ — VERIFIED
- n=904 workers with ADHD symptoms vs 900 controls
- Hybrid work and full WFH associated with lower PHQ-9 (depressive symptoms) vs full on-site in ADHD-symptomatic workers
- WFH not significantly associated with WPAI score or EQ-5D improvement
- Manufacturing/construction job type significantly associated with higher presenteeism
- Confidence: MEDIUM (observational, undiagnosed ADHD by ASRS only, industry-funded)

### T2 DOMAIN: Employment Outcomes, Earnings, Occupational Functioning

**7. Li L et al. 2022 — ADHD Medication and Long-term Unemployment, Sweden**
- PMID: 35476068 | DOI: 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2022.6815 | JAMA Netw Open
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35476068/ — VERIFIED
- Population-based cohort; n=12,875 middle-aged Swedish adults with ADHD (born 1958–1978)
- ADHD medication use associated with 10% lower risk of long-term unemployment (adjusted RR 0.90, 95% CI 0.87–0.95)
- Effect primarily in women (RR 0.82, 95% CI 0.76–0.89); not significant in men (RR 0.96, 0.91–1.01)
- Within-individual comparison: lower unemployment rate during treatment vs non-treatment periods (RR 0.89, 95% CI 0.85–0.94)
- Confidence: HIGH (register-based, quasi-experimental within-individual design)
- Caveat: COI — several authors affiliated with Shire/Takeda

**8. Anker E et al. 2019 — Work Participation in ADHD**
- PMID: 30187383 | DOI: 10.1007/s12402-018-0260-2 | Atten Defic Hyperact Disord
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30187383/ — VERIFIED (2019 paper, pre-range)
- Norwegian clinic sample; higher rates of occupational failure documented
- Lifetime depression and ADHD severity both associated with work non-participation
- Confidence: MEDIUM (clinical sample, cross-sectional)

**9. Chaulagain A et al. 2023 — Systematic Meta-Review of ADHD Systematic Reviews**
- PMID: 37974470 | DOI: 10.1192/j.eurpsy.2023.2451 | Eur Psychiatry
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37974470/ — VERIFIED
- n=231 systematic reviews and meta-analyses reviewed
- Adult ADHD prevalence 2.5% (note: high uncertainty)
- Strong evidence for stimulant efficacy on symptom reduction; limited evidence that pharmacotherapy mitigates adverse life trajectories (employment, education, substance abuse, crime)
- Non-pharmacological treatment efficacy: "mixed"
- Confidence: HIGH as a meta-review

**10. Voyer C et al. 2025 — Occupational Intervention for QoL in ADHD (RCT Protocol)**
- PMID: 40602661 | DOI: 10.1016/j.cct.2025.107997 | Contemp Clin Trials
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40602661/ — VERIFIED
- 8-week virtual intervention for workplace QoL in adults with possible ADHD
- Protocol only (NCT06774378); outcomes pending
- Confidence: N/A (protocol, no results yet)

### T3 DOMAIN: Clinical EF Interventions — CBT, Coaching, Mindfulness

**11. Liu Y et al. 2026 — Meta-analysis of CBT for Adult ADHD**
- PMID: 41483880 | DOI: 10.1016/j.jad.2025.121107 | J Affect Disord
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41483880/ — VERIFIED
- 14 RCTs from 949 records
- Core ADHD symptoms: SMD = −0.45 (P < 0.00001)
- Executive function: SMD = −0.43 (P = 0.001)
- Depression: SMD = −0.23 (P = 0.001)
- Anxiety/stress: SMD = −0.24 (P = 0.003)
- QoL: SMD = 0.12 (NS); self-evaluation: SMD = −0.30 (NS)
- Pure CBT: superior on core symptoms (SMD −0.51) and EF (SMD −0.56)
- CBT + medication: greater benefit for QoL and anxiety
- Group CBT superior for core symptoms/EF; individual CBT superior for emotional outcomes
- Confidence: HIGH (meta-analysis of RCTs, Cochrane methodology)

**12. Zhang LQ et al. 2026 — DBT vs CBT RCT for Adults with ADHD (China)**
- PMID: 42106651 | DOI: 10.1186/s12888-026-08130-w | BMC Psychiatry
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42106651/ — VERIFIED
- n=98 adults, 12-week group CBT vs group DBT; 6-month follow-up
- Both improved core ADHD symptoms and secondary outcomes
- Between-group d = 0.06 post-treatment; d = 0.17 at 6-month follow-up (non-inferior margin d=0.40 not met)
- CBT advantage on spatial working memory task; DBT not inferior on most measures
- Confidence: HIGH (RCT, 6-month follow-up)

**13. Lopez PL et al. 2018 — Cochrane Review: CBT for Adult ADHD**
- PMID: 29566425 | DOI: 10.1002/14651858.CD010840.pub2 | Cochrane Database Syst Rev
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29566425/ — VERIFIED
- 14 RCTs, n=700 participants
- CBT vs waitlist: clinician-reported symptoms (SMD −1.22, 95% CI −2.03 to −0.41, very low quality)
- CBT vs waitlist: self-reported (SMD −0.84, 95% CI −1.18 to −0.50, moderate quality, large effect)
- CBT + pharmacotherapy vs pharmacotherapy alone: SMD −0.80 (large effect, very low quality)
- QoL: no significant difference
- Overall: low-quality evidence of benefit; limited long-term follow-up
- Confidence: HIGH as a Cochrane review (but included studies are low-quality)

**14. Gosling CJ et al. 2025 — Umbrella Review: Benefits and Harms of ADHD Interventions (BMJ)**
- PMID: 41297970 | DOI: 10.1136/bmj-2025-085875 | BMJ
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41297970/ — VERIFIED
- 221 unique intervention–outcome combinations from 47 meta-analytic reports
- In adults: atomoxetine, CBT, methylphenidate (and amphetamines in high-quality trials) showed at least moderate-certainty evidence, medium effect sizes on ADHD symptoms
- Mindfulness in adults: large effect sizes but LOW certainty evidence
- No high-certainty long-term evidence for any intervention
- Online platform: https://ebiadhd-database.org/
- Confidence: HIGH (umbrella review, GRADE-adapted quality assessment)

**15. Nordby ES et al. 2024 — Blended Intervention for Emotion Dysregulation in Adult ADHD**
- PMID: 38231536 | DOI: N/A but at JMIR Formative Research | JMIR Form Res
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38231536/ — (PMID referenced in T4 paper search)
- n=16 adults with ADHD; 8-week DBT skills + digital app companion; uncontrolled pre-post
- Emotion dysregulation d=2.0; inattention d=1.1; hyperactivity-impulsivity d=0.9
- No significant improvement in executive function, depression, anxiety, or QoL
- Adherence: 6.2/8 group sessions, 6.7/8 app modules
- Confidence: LOW (uncontrolled, n=16, pre-post only)

### T4 DOMAIN: Digital Interventions

**16. Antshel KM et al. 2026 — Digital CBT App RCT for Adult ADHD**
- PMID: 41220055 | DOI: 10.1177/10870547251384462 | J Atten Disord
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41220055/ — VERIFIED
- n=154 adults (18–55); 8 weeks; CBT-informed app vs waitlist
- Inattentive symptoms: η² = .15 (significant group × time)
- Hyperactive-impulsive symptoms: η² = .05
- ADHD QoL: η² = .04
- No effect on functional impairment measure
- Symptom reduction positively correlated with total app exercises completed
- COI: co-author Knouse holds stock options in Inflow Holdings (app developer)
- Confidence: MEDIUM (RCT but waitlist control only, not active control)

**17. Selaskowski B et al. 2022 — Smartphone-Assisted Psychoeducation RCT (Germany)**
- PMID: 36041353 | DOI: 10.1016/j.psychres.2022.114802 | Psychiatry Res
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36041353/ — VERIFIED
- n=60 adults with ADHD; smartphone app vs pen-and-paper brochures; 8 weekly sessions
- Both groups showed significant reduction in ADHD core symptoms
- Smartphone-assisted group significantly better on inattention and impulsivity
- Higher homework compliance in smartphone group
- No adverse events
- Confidence: HIGH (RCT, active comparator — active control not waitlist)

**18. Jang S et al. 2021 — Mobile Chatbot CBT for Adult Attention Deficit (Korea)**
- PMID: 33799055 | DOI: 10.1016/j.ijmedinf.2021.104440 | Int J Med Inform
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33799055/ — VERIFIED
- n=46 adults with attention deficit (19–60 years); "Todaki" chatbot vs information-only control; 4 weeks
- Significant group × time reduction in ADHD symptoms (hyperactive-impulsive F=4.39, P=.04; total symptoms F=6.74, P=.01) in chatbot group
- Average chatbot use: 20.32 times / 1h15m per 4 weeks
- Symptom improvement correlated with psychoeducation module usage
- Confidence: LOW-MEDIUM (pilot, n=46, "attention deficit" not formally diagnosed ADHD, single-blind)

**19. Carvalho LR et al. 2023 — FOCUS ADHD App RCT (Brazil)**
- PMID: 37341028 | DOI: 10.1192/j.eurpsy.2023.2422 | Eur Psychiatry
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37341028/ — VERIFIED
- n=73 adults; FOCUS ADHD app vs TAU vs app + financial incentive; 3 months
- No significant difference in medication adherence (MPR) across groups
- App + financial incentive produced 100% adoption rate and higher medication intake registration
- App usability rated favorably
- Confidence: MEDIUM (RCT but primary outcome null; adherence measure narrow)

**20. Pasarelu CR et al. 2020 — Systematic Review of ADHD Mobile Apps**
- PMID: 32283479 | DOI: 10.1016/j.ijmedinf.2020.104133 | Int J Med Inform
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32283479/ — VERIFIED
- Found majority of apps target pediatric population; evidence base for adult apps very limited
- Most apps lack RCT evidence; primary focus on psychoeducation, reminders, symptom tracking
- Confidence: MEDIUM (systematic review but evidence base reviewed is weak)

### Cross-Cutting / Epidemiology

**21. Song P et al. 2021 — Global Prevalence of Adult ADHD: Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis**
- PMID: 33692893 | DOI: 10.7189/jogh.11.04009 | J Glob Health
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33692893/ — VERIFIED
- Persistent adult ADHD (childhood onset): 2.58% globally (≈139.84M affected in 2020)
- Symptomatic adult ADHD (regardless of childhood onset): 6.76% (≈366.33M in 2020)
- Prevalence decreases with age
- Confidence: HIGH (systematic review + meta-analysis, 2020 global demographic adjustment)

**22. Faraone SV et al. 2021 — World Federation of ADHD International Consensus Statement**
- PMID: 33549739 | DOI: 10.1016/j.neubiorev.2021.01.022 | Neurosci Biobehav Rev
- URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33549739/ — VERIFIED
- 208 empirically supported statements, endorsed by 80 authors from 27 countries
- Confirms ADHD persistence into adulthood, occupational/functional impairment, comorbidity burden
- Stimulant efficacy for core symptoms well-supported; long-term outcomes less certain
- Confidence: HIGH (consensus, meta-analysis threshold requirement)
