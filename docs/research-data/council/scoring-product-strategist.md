# Product Strategist — RICE Scoring

> **Perspective**: Market fit, differentiation, competitive positioning
> **Core question**: What makes Aether worth switching to?
> **Target segment**: Knowledge workers drowning in tabs who need AI but don't trust it
> **Launch headline**: "The browser that thinks in projects, not tabs — with AI that never leaves your machine."

## Scoring Principles

1. **Differentiation premium**: Features no other browser offers get confidence and impact boosts. Table stakes get effort-discounted but not inflated.
2. **Switching incentive**: 81% willing to switch, but inertia is real. Only features that create a "holy shit" moment in the first 5 minutes matter for reach.
3. **Compounding value**: Features that make other features more valuable score higher. Workspaces + AI context + session persistence = more than sum of parts.
4. **Narrative coherence**: The launch story must be a single sentence. Scattered features = no story = no press = no users.

## Rationale for Top 10

### 1. Project-First Workspaces (RICE 5.79, Must)
This IS Aether's identity. Arc proved the appetite; its death proved the vacuum. No browser treats projects as first-class entities with scoped state, cookies, and history. This is the single feature that makes the first-5-minutes experience feel fundamentally different from Chrome. Every other feature (AI context, session persistence, tab lifecycle) compounds on top of this. Without it, Aether is "another browser with AI" — with it, Aether is "the browser that understands what I'm working on." High effort, but non-negotiable.

### 2. Local/On-Device AI Inference (RICE 4.00, Must)
The single strongest differentiator in the AI browser space. Every competitor sends your data to the cloud. 52% of AI extensions collect data. "AI that never leaves your machine" is a one-sentence pitch that writes itself. This resolves the defining tension of the market: users want AI but don't trust it. Confidence is 0.8 (not 1.0) because on-device model quality is still behind cloud, and hardware requirements limit reach. But the strategic value is enormous — it makes the privacy claim credible in a way no policy document can.

### 3. Native Ad/Tracker Blocking (RICE 10.00, Must)
Highest raw RICE score because it's trivially high reach (900M+ ad-block users), proven impact, near-certain confidence, and modest effort (well-understood engineering). But it's table stakes, not differentiation — Brave, Firefox, and every privacy browser already have this. I score it Must because without it, Aether fails the "at least as good as what I have" test. Users won't switch TO ad blocking, but they'll refuse to switch WITHOUT it.

### 4. Native Vim/Modal Keybindings (RICE 3.75, Must)
Narrow reach (5) but maximum impact (5) for the target segment. This is the most underserved, loudest, most evangelistic user base in software: 660K+ keyboard-first users with no good option after Vimperator died. Extensions structurally cannot solve this (WebExtension sandbox blocks browser chrome access). These users write blog posts, make YouTube videos, and convince their teams. They are the beachhead: high switching cost tolerance, high word-of-mouth coefficient. The entire keyboard system (command palette, shortcuts, browser chrome control) compounds from this foundation.

### 5. Agent Automation (RICE 2.00, Should)
Lower RICE due to high effort (9) and low confidence (0.6) — current reliability is 19.7% for 10-step workflows. But strategically critical: WebMCP in Chrome 146 means Google is coming for this space. If Aether doesn't have a credible agent story at launch, we're fighting Google on their turf later. The agent platform is where developer ecosystem lock-in happens. Should, not Must, because the technology isn't ready for a reliable launch — better to ship a solid foundation (Native Agent API + debugging tools) than overpromise.

### 6. LLM Sidebar Chat (RICE 6.48, Must)
High RICE because broad reach, moderate effort. Every AI browser has this; its absence would be conspicuous. But the differentiator isn't the sidebar itself — it's the combination with local inference and workspace context. The sidebar fed by on-device AI, scoped to the current project, remembering what you discussed yesterday — that's the product. The sidebar alone is commodity.

### 7. Vertical Tab Sidebar (RICE 9.00, Must)
Second-highest raw RICE. Chrome just shipped this after 17 years. It's table stakes for 2026, but it's also the visual scaffold for workspaces and tree tabs. Without vertical tabs, the workspace switching UX has no natural home. Low effort, high reach, certain impact. Ship it.

### 8. Per-Workspace Session Persistence (RICE 5.04, Must)
The feature that makes workspaces real instead of cosmetic. Arc had "spaces" but they were shallow — Aether's workspaces with full session state (scroll positions, form data, authentication state) become the reason you can't go back to Chrome. This is the lock-in feature: once you have 5 projects with preserved state, switching cost becomes prohibitive. That's the goal.

### 9. Prompt Injection Defense (RICE 3.20, Should)
Not sexy, but existential for the AI-native positioning. One viral "browser AI reads your bank page and sends credentials to attacker" story kills the product. OpenAI's CISO calls this "frontier, unsolved" — which means being first with a credible defense is massive differentiation. Should rather than Must because the defense can be progressive: ship with conservative defaults (require user confirmation for cross-origin AI access) and build toward architectural isolation.

### 10. WebExtension API Support (RICE 6.00, Must)
The ultimate table stakes feature. Without your existing extensions, you won't switch. Period. Reach is 10 because it affects literally every user. But it's zero differentiation — it's just not being worse than Firefox. Must because the migration path requires it; uBlock Origin, 1Password, Bitwarden — these are non-negotiable.

## Controversial Picks

### Vim Keybindings as "Must" (Rank 4)
Other council members will argue this is niche (660K users vs. billions of browser users). They're wrong about the strategic math. Early-stage products don't need mass reach — they need fanatical adopters who do your marketing for free. Vim users are the most vocal, most technically sophisticated, most blog-writing demographic in software. They have been explicitly abandoned by every browser since Firefox killed XUL. The switching incentive is not "slightly better" — it's "finally possible." That's a different kind of reach.

### Agent Automation as "Should" not "Must" (Rank 5)
Agent builders will scream. But at 19.7% reliability for 10-step workflows, shipping this as a headline feature is a credibility risk. Better to ship the foundation (API surface, debugging tools, MCP integration) and let the reliability improve iteratively. The narrative should be "the browser built for agents" with the developer tools to prove it, not "watch our agent fail on step 7."

### AI Page Summarization as "Should" not "Must" (Rank 11)
It's the #1 AI extension use case with broad reach. But it's commodity — every AI browser has it, and standalone tools (ChatGPT, Kagi) do it better than any browser sidebar. It's not a switching reason. Ship it, but don't lead with it.

### Cross-Device Sync as "Should" (Rank 13)
Users expect it, but it's table stakes AND high effort AND directly conflicts with "zero telemetry." Privacy-preserving sync is a hard engineering problem. For launch, excellent single-device experience + manual export/import is acceptable. Sync is a retention feature, not an acquisition feature — you need it by month 3, not day 1.

### MCP Server as "Should" (Rank 36)
WebMCP in Chrome 146 means this protocol is going mainstream. Aether-as-MCP-server lets every AI tool (Cursor, Claude, etc.) use Aether as its browser. This is the ecosystem play: Aether becomes the browser that AI tools prefer. Scored lower on reach (only developers care today) but the strategic value of being the "AI's browser" is enormous. The disagreement will be whether this is Must or Could — I say Should because the MCP ecosystem is still forming.

### Theming as "Could" (Rank 42)
92% want personalization, but themes don't make anyone switch browsers. They make people who already switched feel at home. It's a retention feature dressed up in high survey numbers. Ship it eventually, don't prioritize it over the core loop.
